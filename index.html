Women and Equality, by Margaret Cowl

By Audrey Funk

What is the Women and Equality archive?

The archive that I chose is a pamphlet titled Women and Equity by Margaret Cowl. It was written in 1935 by the Worker’s Library Publishers and is now a part of the University of Pittsburgh’s American Left Ephemera Collection. It is only 14 pages long but it covers an important topic surrounding women's rights. It argues that women deserve equal treatment not only in politics but also in the work space, in family life, and in society in general.

The pamphlet looks small and simple, but it documents the idea that women’s equality is tied into worker’s rights and inequalities from social class. By adding gender equality into this bigger picture of economic and social inequalities, Cowl presents a strong argument that women’s rights violations cannot be separated from the overall push to equality in the larger society.

What is in the archive?

This piece was made to influence its readers and spread awareness. Margaret Cowl published this pamphlet in 1935, during the Great Depression, when many people were unemployed. This social change felt urgent during this time of need. Worker’s Library Publishers, the company that printed this pamphlet, was mainly known for supporting democratic and communist causes. The goal was to reach all kinds of people through easy-to-read and easy-to-share pamphlets that could be handed out at meetings, rallies or on the street.

In Women in Equity, Cowl talks about issues that are still apparent today. She describes how women faced discrimination in jobs and in life. There was very little protections for pregnant women, and the laws against birth control violated women's health and their independence. She connected these struggles to a larger inequality of capitalism and she argued that true equality cannot exist until the deeper rooted issues within society are exposed.

This pamphlet also has a connection to other Pitt archives. There are many writings about feminism and sexuality like “Women, War and Facism” and “The Position of Negro Women.” These documents display 1930s activists' opinions on not just gender inequalities, but also race, class, and sexuality. When read together, they provide the larger picture of social inequalities and reform from decades ago.

Why is this important?

This pamphlet is important because it shows how women's rights have been debated for so long. Cowl’s writing proves that activists have been connecting different issues to each other even back in the 1930s. Her writing shows us unique experiences and perspectives that textbooks don’t. She wrote about many issues at once, proving that the conversation was much more complicated and started earlier than most people know.

Also, many secondary sources back up what Cowl argued in interesting ways. According to Morgan (1989), during the Depression, lots of women went against the common idea that their place was in the home. The United Nations (n.d) even emphasized that gender equality is not just a political issue, but a human right. They believed women’s rights were necessary for peace and development. These sources both show how early feminist ideas were relevant, but also how much work remained.

Another perspective is from the National Intelligence Council (2021), which believes that while women have gained legal rights in many countries, economic and social backlash and uneven progress shows that gender equality can slow or stop at any moment. This aligns with what Cowl expressed about obstacles. Her pamphlet speaks to social norms and legal change, not just one or the other.

Analyzing Women and Equality raises many new questions. One question about Margaret Cowl herself. Who was she and how did she become an important voice for women’s rights? Another question about the pamphlet’s influence. Did it change people’s minds at all? Was it read widely, or only seen in small groups?

For researchers, pamphlets like this one open up new paths of study. Comparing Women and Equality to other documents from the 1930s can show how women’s rights were debated across many different races, ages, and political parties. It also allows the comparison between American activists and European activists. Moran’s (1989) article suggests that even though there was vocal resistance to women’s rights violations, there was a “void” that many aims were not being fought for or against.

For the archive itself, the next step is to continue copying and preserving pamphlets like this one. Having documents like this online and at easy access makes it easier for the public to see and use them. Adding notes, timelines, and annotations also make the pamphlet more understandable for people who are not historians or professionals.

Finally, for students, this pamphlet is a good reminder to question even simple stories in history. Instead of thinking about women’s rights as a step by step progression, it is easy to see that activists were always debating and sometimes were far ahead of their time. Cowl’s pamphlet proves that the fight for equality is a long and complicated story to which the end has not yet been written.
